Traditional African Artists

Traditional African artists were skilled craftspeople deeply integrated into community life, serving as cultural preservers and transmitters of knowledge.

Characteristics of Traditional Artists

  • Training: Learned through informal apprenticeship systems
  • Heritage: Often hereditary professions passed down through families
  • Function: Created functional and ceremonial objects for community use
  • Tradition: Worked within established cultural traditions and aesthetic frameworks
  • Recognition: Respected for their unique abilities that set them apart from the community
  • Role: Served as translators of traditional ideas, philosophy, and beliefs into visual form

Community Functions

Cultural Documentation

Record and document ideas, events, and cultural knowledge through artistic expression

Spiritual Mediation

Create objects for religious ceremonies, rituals, and spiritual practices

Social Cohesion

Produce works that strengthen community bonds and transmit cultural values

Practical Creation

Make domestic implements, farm tools, and everyday functional objects

Gender Roles in African Art

Male Artists

Traditional Materials & Specializations

  • Metalwork: Blacksmithing, brass casting, silversmithing
  • Wood Carving: Sculpture, masks, furniture, implements
  • Stone & Ivory: Carving and shaping hard materials
  • Architecture: Building construction and decoration
  • Weapons: Crafting tools and military equipment

Regional Examples

  • Bamana (Mali): Male blacksmiths create religious sculptures
  • Tuareg: Male blacksmiths and silversmiths specialize in jewelry
  • Asante (Ghana): Male craftsmen work in gold and brass
  • Hausa (Nigeria): Men work in leather and metalwork

Female Artists

Traditional Materials & Specializations

  • Pottery: Clay vessels, ceremonial and functional ceramics
  • Textiles: Weaving, dyeing, embroidery, fabric decoration
  • Beadwork: Jewelry, adornment, ceremonial accessories
  • Body Art: Hairstyling, scarification, body painting
  • Wall Decoration: House painting and mural creation

Regional Examples

  • Ewe (Ghana): Women are the primary pottery makers
  • Luba: Women create carved objects in dried camwood paste
  • Yoruba: Women produce commemorative figures in clay
  • Tuareg: Women work in animal skin and leather
  • Swahili: Women create printed textiles and kanga designs

Marriage and Craft Partnerships

In many African societies, artistic specializations are linked through marriage patterns:

  • Bamana (Mali): Female potters traditionally marry male blacksmiths
  • Brass Casters: Women potters often marry male brass casters
  • Nupe (Nigeria): Women potters in Bida are married to male glass bead makers
  • Complementary Skills: These partnerships create households with diverse artistic capabilities

Gender Role Changes & Adaptations

Historical Role Reversals

Traditional gender roles in African art have evolved due to social, economic, and religious changes:

Hausa Pottery (Nigeria)

Traditional: Women were the primary pottery makers

Change: Islamic practices of female seclusion led men to take over pottery production

Innovation: Men introduced new techniques like concave mould methods and large earthen kilns

Recent Trend: Women are returning to pottery as men pursue more lucrative jobs

Indigo Dyeing

Traditional: Primarily a female specialization in West Africa

19th Century: Men took over indigo dyeing in some regions

Modern: Both men and women participate in textile dyeing

Aluminum Smithing

Traditional: Male specialization

Modern: Women now take active roles in aluminum smithing centers

Division: Men shape through hammering, women focus on decoration

Contemporary African Artists

Formally Trained Artists

Contemporary African artists receive formal education and work within both local and international contexts.

Characteristics

  • Education: Formal training from Western-styled institutions
  • Expression: Individual artistic styles alongside community aesthetic values
  • Purpose: Create works for aesthetic enjoyment and cultural commentary
  • Freedom: Ability to depart from traditional aesthetic constraints
  • Markets: Work for local and international art markets
  • Community: Part of local and global artistic communities

Categories of Contemporary Artists

Traditional Continuity Artists

Continue traditional pre-colonial practices while adapting to contemporary contexts

Popular Artists

Self-taught artists creating works for local and tourist markets

International Artists

Academically trained artists targeting global art markets and addressing contemporary issues

Contemporary Themes & Concerns

  • Cultural Identity: Exploring relationships between ancestral past and modern present
  • Social Issues: Addressing corruption, environment, waste, and political concerns
  • Media Innovation: Experimenting with new materials and technologies
  • Global Dialogue: Participating in international exhibitions and biennales
  • Urban Themes: Reflecting city life and modern African experiences
